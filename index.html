Ronin Toys

Current Work (September 2025–Present)

I run Ronin Toys, where I design and manufacture cat toys. Right now that's the Reelin (a fishing-rod style toy) and Rattletail (a chaseable toy for cats that doubles up as a fidget toy for humans), both 3D-printed in PLA+ plastic. They're durable, they're fun, and cats lose their minds over them in the best possible way.

This is what happens when you take years of designing narratives and experiences and apply it to making actual objects. At Byju's, I designed learning modules for 3-5 year olds—the whole brief was "make education feel like play." Turns out, designing play experiences for cats uses a lot of the same thinking: what holds attention, what creates joy, what makes you want to do it again.

Why This Works Right Now

India's toy market has opened up in interesting ways. There's space for small-batch, thoughtfully designed products that aren't just trying to be cheaper than everything else. Pet parents are investing in quality. And manufacturing—whether it's 3D printing, woodworking, or textile production—is more accessible than it's been before. You can actually make things without needing a factory or a pile of venture capital.

I'm building something that's economically sustainable and built to last. Not trying to scale to the moon, just making good work that works.

The Wood Vision (And Why I'm Talking About It)

The Reelin is moving to wood—properly finished, properly made, the kind of object that lasts and doesn't end up in a landfill. Getting there means building a network of artisans: woodworkers and carpenters who know their craft and want to make things that matter.

I'm working on building that network now. The model I'm aiming for puts at least 30% of profits directly to the artisans—not as charity, but as fair payment for skilled work. This only works if the economics work, which means figuring out pricing, distribution, and whether people will pay for wood toys when plastic is so much cheaper.

That's the direction. I'm figuring it out as I go, which honestly makes it more interesting.

How I'm Building This

Right now, everything is self-funded and friend-powered. I've been learning 3D printing and manufacturing with support from Kailash at Ampere Works, and friends have helped with web design, social media, and product development. The overhead is low, I'm doing as much as I can myself, and I'm treating this as a long-term build, not a sprint.

I'm also prototyping other products—like the Ragdoll range, which will be made from upcycled, donated fabric—but those are still in development. For now, the focus is on getting the Reelin and Rattletail right, building the customer base, and establishing the artisan connections that will make the wood transition possible.

Why India, Why 2025

There's something happening in India right now around manufacturing, maker culture, and small-batch production. It's not just about cost anymore—people are willing to pay for quality, for thoughtful design, for things that last. The infrastructure is there: 3D printing services, woodworking communities, textile artisans, all accessible in ways they weren't even five years ago.

And the pet market is growing. People care about what they're buying for their cats. They're looking for toys that aren't just cheap plastic nonsense imported from elsewhere. There's space for something local, well-made, and designed with care.

This isn't about disrupting anything. It's about building something sustainable that values craft, fair work, and quality. It's optimistic about what's possible in India right now—not naive, just paying attention to what's actually happening on the ground.

What Would Help

I'm running this myself, and that's not changing. But there are specific things that would help this grow in the right direction:

  • Funding — Trusts, NGOs, grant programs, or people interested in supporting ethical manufacturing and maker networks. I'm not looking for equity investment; I'm looking for funding that understands infrastructure-building and public good. This is about building artisan networks and transitioning to sustainable materials, not about exits or scale.
  • Connections — Artisans working with wood or upcycled fabric. People who've built artisan networks before and can share what actually works. Anyone who knows how to structure small-batch manufacturing sustainably. I need practical knowledge from people who've done this, not just theories about how it might work.
  • Orders — If you have cats (or know people who do), the toys are here. Word of mouth goes a long way. Every order helps prove the model, refine the products, and build toward the wood transition.

If any of that resonates with you—or if you just want to talk about what I'm building—reach out at ruphilka@gmail.com. I'm always up for a good conversation.

How This Fits Together

I've spent years doing corporate work: internal communications for Lenovo, brand voice for Maersk and Sealand, social media strategy for Licious, narrative design for Byju's. I know how to manage campaigns, write for global audiences, and make complex things make sense. That work taught me a lot, and I'm good at it.

Ronin Toys is me taking all of that—the strategy, the rigor, the storytelling—and building something I actually want to see in the world. It's not anti-corporate, it's just... parallel. A different way of working that doesn't feel like a compromise.

This is about making things that matter: good toys, fair work, community networks. It's about taking 14 years of professional experience and using it to build something tangible, something that exists in the world and makes it slightly better.
